# Cyclical Stock Valuation Pitfalls

## The Core Problem

Using raw P/E to judge whether a cyclical stock is "cheap" or "expensive" is backwards:

- **Cycle peak:** Earnings are at all-time highs → P/E appears very LOW (e.g., Micron at 9x) → looks "cheap" but is actually EXPENSIVE because earnings will mean-revert
- **Cycle trough:** Earnings are depressed or negative → P/E appears very HIGH or negative → looks "expensive" but is actually CHEAP because earnings will recover

This is the classic value trap — low P/E at cycle peaks is the market rationally discounting unsustainable earnings, not an undervaluation signal.

## Correct Valuation Methods for Cyclicals

1. **Normalized Earnings P/E** (primary): Use 5-10 year median EPS (or average across a full cycle), NOT current peak/trough EPS
2. **P/B + ROE framework** (secondary): Low P/B + high ROE = potentially undervalued. High P/B + declining ROE = potentially overvalued
3. **Cross-cycle anchors**: Always compare against S&P 500 median P/E (~20x) as a sanity check — if the entire sector is in a bubble, sector median is also inflated

## Pitfalls of Common Shortcuts

| Shortcut | Why It Fails |
|----------|-------------|
| 3-year historical P/E range | Too short — may only capture one phase of the cycle |
| Sector median comparison | In a sector-wide bubble, the median is also inflated |
| Forward P/E alone | Analyst estimates lag reality at cycle turns; trailing vs forward P/E diverging >3x signals fragile earnings base |
| Ignoring growth rates | P/E 30x growing 50% is cheaper than P/E 15x growing 5%. Use PEG as a cross-check |

## When P/E Breaks Entirely

- **Negative earnings** → P/E is meaningless. Fall back to P/B, P/S, or EV/Revenue
- **P/E > 100x** → Near-zero earnings base, P/E number is noise
- **Trailing vs forward P/E diverge by >3x** → Earnings base is fragile, forward P/E depends on strong assumptions

## Summary Rule

For any cyclical stock, never present raw P/E as the primary valuation signal without:
1. A normalized earnings calculation (or explicit note that it's unavailable)
2. A cross-cycle anchor (S&P 500)
3. A warning that low P/E at cycle peaks may be a trap, not an opportunity
